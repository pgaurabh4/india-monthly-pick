## The One-Line Thesis

Kaynes is building India's full electronics value chain — from assembling circuit boards to packaging semiconductors — and the market has punished it 59% from its highs over short-term margin noise, giving you a rare entry into a decade-long structural compounder at a reasonable price.

---

## Why This Company, Why Now

India has a problem and an ambition. The problem: we import nearly everything electronic. Every phone, every EV controller, every defence radar sub-assembly, every industrial IoT sensor — almost all of it comes from China or Taiwan. The ambition: change this completely over the next decade. The government has committed ₹2+ lakh crore in PLI schemes, semiconductor missions, and defence indigenisation budgets to make it happen.

Kaynes is one of the very few companies in India positioned to capture this end-to-end. They don't just assemble electronics for someone else's design. They build complex, high-mix, low-volume electronics — the kind that goes inside aircraft, defence systems, medical devices, and industrial machinery. This is a fundamentally different and more valuable business than churning out mass-market smartphones. Their EBITDA margins of 15–16% prove it — commodity EMS players make 4–6%.

And now they have done something no other Indian company has done: they have commissioned India's second semiconductor packaging (OSAT) facility at Sanand, Gujarat. PM Modi inaugurated it. They have already shipped commercially to a Tier-1 global semiconductor customer. This is not a plan on paper — it is operational. The semiconductor unit is currently loss-making as it ramps up, which is precisely why the stock is down 59% from its highs. The market is pricing in the pain and ignoring the prize.

## Why It Is NOT a Value Trap

A value trap is a cheap stock that stays cheap because the business is structurally declining or has no earnings power. Kaynes is the opposite. Here is the checklist:

**Revenue growing 33% — verified from FY26 audited results.** Not a one-off. FY25 was also 30%+ growth. The order book growth (60% in FY25) is the leading indicator and it is accelerating.

**Margins are suppressed temporarily, not permanently.** The OSAT facility at Sanand cost ₹3,300 Cr to build. During the ramp phase — which is happening now — it runs at a loss. Once utilisation crosses breakeven (estimated 18–24 months from now), it adds high-margin semiconductor revenue on top of the existing EMS business. Every EMS company in the world that built OSAT went through this exact J-curve. TSMC did. ASE did. Kaynes is doing it now at India scale.

**The de-rating is explained by capex pain, not business deterioration.** Q4 FY26 PAT fell 21% QoQ because of OSAT ramp costs. The market sold off. The underlying business — EMS revenues, order wins, customer additions — continued growing. When temporary pain causes permanent-looking price damage, that is an opportunity.

**42x forward PE on 30%+ growth is not expensive.** The PEG ratio works out to roughly 1.4x. For a company with a 2.5x revenue order book, 40% gross margins in their specialised segments, and a semiconductor option that is not priced in at all — this is attractive. Dixon Technologies, which is a simpler, lower-margin EMS business, trades at 70x forward earnings. Kaynes deserves at least that multiple once the OSAT ramp costs clear.

## India Growth Story Alignment

This stock ticks every major structural theme in India right now:

**China+1.** Global companies are desperate to move supply chains out of China. India is the only country at scale that can absorb this. Electronics is the largest category. Kaynes is already winning orders from Japanese, European, and American OEMs because of this shift.

**Make in India — Defence.** India's defence budget is at an all-time high and 75% of capital procurement is now mandated to go to domestic companies. Kaynes makes PCBs and electronic assemblies for defence platforms. Every new indigenous weapon system needs their components.

**India Semiconductor Mission.** The government is subsidising 50% of capex for semiconductor facilities. Kaynes received this subsidy for Sanand. India's ambition to have a domestic semiconductor ecosystem is a 20-year policy, not a 2-year scheme. Kaynes is the first mover in OSAT — the most approachable segment of the value chain for Indian industry.

**EV and Automotive Electronics.** India's automotive sector is electrifying. EV controllers, battery management systems, motor drives, ADAS sensors — all of this is electronics that currently gets imported. Kaynes is already supplying to automotive OEMs and this segment will grow significantly as domestic EV production scales.

## What Could Go Wrong

This is a 5–10 year bet, not a 6-month trade. The risks are real and should be understood:

- **OSAT execution risk.** The Sanand facility is ramping but has not hit breakeven. If the first Tier-1 customer does not renew or expand, the ramp could take longer than expected.
- **Automotive slowdown.** Any EV demand slowdown in India affects one of their key segments.
- **Capital intensity.** They have spent ₹3,300 Cr on OSAT and are spending more. Net debt has risen. If revenue growth slows while interest costs rise, PAT could miss expectations further.
- **Management execution.** Scaling from ₹3,600 Cr to ₹10,000 Cr+ revenue while simultaneously managing an OSAT ramp is operationally complex.

None of these risks are thesis-breaking. They are the normal execution risks of a company in aggressive growth mode. The question is whether you believe in the structural opportunity — and on that, the answer is yes.

Current market cap is ₹20,626 Cr. That is a 5–7x return potential over 7 years, or roughly 25–30% CAGR.

This is not a guaranteed outcome. But it is a realistic one for a company that has the order book, the infrastructure, the government backing, and the management track record to get there. And you are buying it at 59% below its highs, when the pain is visible and the prize is not.
